The Language of Fashion

How clothes tell the world who we are

Why Fashion Matters

Fashion is rarely just about clothes. It is one of the oldest forms of human communication, a visual vocabulary that signals belonging, rebellion, aspiration, and identity all at once. Long before someone speaks, what they wear has already said something.

Growing up in Southern California, I was surrounded by a specific aesthetic: sun-bleached denim, effortless layers, the kind of style that looked like it required no effort but actually required a lot. It made me curious about how regional culture shapes the clothes people reach for, and how fashion houses translate those instincts into something global.

Texture and drape — the fabric is the message

What I love about it

Fashion sits at the intersection of art, business, psychology, and culture. It is one of the few industries where creative vision and commercial instinct have to coexist in the same room at the same time.

That tension is exactly what makes it interesting to study from a business perspective: who decides what gets made, how it reaches people, and why some things become iconic while others disappear.

A Brief History of Fashion Eras

Every decade reacts to the one before it. Below is a quick guide to some of the most defining periods in modern fashion, with the key silhouettes and cultural forces that shaped each one.

  1. 1920s — Liberation
    • Drop-waist dresses and cloche hats
    • Women's hemlines rose for the first time in history
    • Coco Chanel popularized jersey fabric for everyday wear
  2. 1950s — Structure
    • Dior's "New Look": nipped waists and full skirts
    • Postwar optimism expressed through elegance
    • Gloves, hats, and matching sets as daily dress codes
  3. 1970s — Freedom
    • Bell-bottoms, bohemian prints, and earth tones
    • Counterculture movement pushed fashion off the runway and into the streets
    • Diane von Furstenberg's wrap dress redefined working women's style
  4. 1990s — Minimalism meets Maximalism
    • Calvin Klein's clean lines ran alongside Versace's bold prints
    • Supermodel era: fashion became celebrity
    • Grunge entered the runways via Marc Jacobs at Perry Ellis
  5. 2020s — Fluidity
    • Gender-neutral and size-inclusive collections become mainstream
    • Resale and vintage go from niche to dominant
    • Social media collapses the distance between trend creation and trend adoption

Style is personal

One of the most interesting things about fashion is how personal it is even when it's mass-produced. Two people can buy the same jacket and wear it completely differently. The garment becomes a starting point, not a conclusion.

That's why I'm drawn to marketing within fashion specifically. The challenge isn't just "how do we sell this?" It's "how do we make someone feel like this was made for them?"
